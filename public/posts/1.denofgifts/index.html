i. the den of gifts

February 9, 2026 · James Cody Phelan

by James Cody Phelan
\

A clamor rang through the morning quiet. Gulls sprung from their roosts above drystone homes and squawked in protest, their days ruined. One squabble of birds drifted over to investigate the commotion and spied a human, only just passing through the dying Koumaria shrubs bundled at the northern edge of the village. The human labored, dragging anger behind him.

Down below, the Goodwoman Sonja knelt before an apricot sapling inspecting the plant for blight. She wiped smeary sweat from her eyes to get a clear look. One of the roots dangled limply but bore no mark of the dreaded rot. As she prepared to set the sapling in the earth, her attention snagged on the noise which had so bothered the seagulls. She left the promising tree, dusted off her dress, and poked her head out around the inlet of her garden wall in curiosity. Other women, stooped women with sun-crinkled faces, quit their sweeping to investigate the noise as well, their husbands and fathers and sons were out at sea, having left well before dawn for the morning catch. Sonja met the gaze of her neighbor across the path. They shared a moment, the two women, each raising a brow in concern before returning their attention up.

Up the cobbles, too distant to make clear, she spotted a figure moving at a sluggish pace. Sonja’s eyes, even once cleared of the hot day’s drippings, had been failing her for some years, and the silhouette above was not known to her. She could, however, judge what he dragged with him.

The anchor was massive, larger than any she had ever seen in a lifetime among fishermen. The man – or adolescent, she now realized – he gripped the broad stock in front of him and snaked backwards over the stones, his bare feet tripping on protruding rock. Gaps in the path threw the anchor momentarily in the air before rising to meet its grating screech. The person in front of the hunk of metal fought for control under the strain.

As he, the anchor, and the gulls drew closer, Sonja covered her ears to drown out the chaos at her doorstep. Only then, fingers plunged into her ear canals, did she recognize the figure. It was the orchard boy, from the farm north of the village. The realization wearied her. Her hands lowered so they could form a tight grasp before her skirt. The cacophony returned. The sun burned hot overhead. Her gaze followed her hands down, and without thinking, she began to rub the creases of her right hand with the thumb of her left, back and forth, her touch feeling the deep crevasses of her worn palm. She brought her gaze up, only to see that the boy and his lonely parade had passed. Out again she peered around the edge of the garden. The boy continued in front of the anchor, sweat sticking his thick black curls to his forehead and nape.

Snapping to her senses, she made to call out, “You mus…” but her voice tumbled over the bridge of her throat in a rasp. Again, voiced, “You mustn’t!”

If he heard her over the noise, he offered no acknowledgement. His focus wrapped a heavy skein around the anchor held fast in his strained arms.

“Goodness!

Child!

Stop, please!”

She was sure that her voice, not known to be meek, had reached him, yet the boy did nothing but pause for a single moment, turn around with some difficulty, and load the anchor’s weight above his shoulder blades. The head of the metal shank protruded past his head, forcing him to look only down as he lumbered forward.

The rock formation, known locally as the Salt Mistress, jutted out from the beach just east of the village’s dock. Like the figurehead on a massive ship, the Mistress had seemingly grown out from the low cliffs on her journey out to the sea. Shallow puddles dotted the beach on either side of the promontory. Over the course of the year, the puddles and the levees which protected them would allow the sun to steal away their water, leaving only salt. For now, though, the puddles sat half full.

Old-man Palinkos sat on top of the Salt Mistress, a fishing rod in his hands, each missing a finger or two. He would never admit it to his wife, but the Mistress was his second love. Every morning since he had stopped going out on the boats, he carried his self-fashioned rod and stool to the rocks and cast uncaringly into the lapping waves below. He often thought about the sea or the gulls or the wind; today, he thought about the salt holes. They were ugly little pocks, really. The beach flowed so natural as it crumbled from the cliffs above and out into the sea, and the Mistress, with her grace and jagged power, gave the cove character. The holes spoiled her grace.

His presence, he now realized, was just the same as the puddles. Atop the rocks, he was no better than their blight against the perfection of the beach.

Oh well. The sun takes from us all - the puddles, their moisture, and me, my skin and bones. Soon I will dry up and the puddles will die.

A screech brought him out of his ruminations. Down the rocks and over the ponds, up past the dock and toward the village, children were fluttering, some dancing or cartwheeling in celebration while others ducked between shaded alcoves in frightful curiosity; dogs yipped and yapped in every direction, uncertain of where to point their ire; gulls and village women observed overhead from their windows.

The young man at the center of the chaos appeared oblivious to the whole affair; his eyes bored down on the path beneath him and saw nothing else. He ambled along in short, controlled, and powerful bursts with his back facing Palinkos and the rock. The fisherman witnesses the entire affair clearly as if watching a play from high up, as like an amphitheater. Only now, the players in the scene acted way up in the rafters, and he, the singular audience member, sat center stage.

After pausing for a half-dozen heartbeats, the boy began the climb down the stairs. There were one hundred and twenty-seven steps from the last precipice of the village to the foot of the docks. This, Palinkos knew, not from ever counting but just as a matter of fact. He also knew that the stairs had been carved from the cliffs long ago. Half were taller than his knees, others barely steps at all.

“You descend the steps of giants and mice! Which are you?” Palinkos shouted at the boy, knowing full well the gibe would not reach his ears. Still, he felt pleased with his wit. His gleeful smirk quickly faded as he, for the first time catching the truth of the scene, saw the parade and commotion for what they were. The crowd, led by children, carried an air of playful curiosity, but the boy’s face, a face twisted with anguish, betrayed their mirth. Sweat poured down from his forehead in viscous drops and drenched his simple shirt along its length. The anchor could not be dragged down the larger steps, so, Palinkos saw, the poor creature improvised a complicated motion. The boy positioned himself with one foot on two successive steps and drew himself close with bent knees. Summoning strength, he then used locked arms and a tense swivel to lift the hulking metal briefly into the air and down onto the step below. A breath, one, two. Then again, he repeated the process, skipping the steps intended for Palinkos’ mice.

Atop the Mistress, the aged fisherman – now standing – watched in horror. Each step curdled his stomach. The anchor dwarfed the man-child, and each crashing drop exposed its impossible weight. The visage before him, coming now into further focus, told only of pain. Up above, the village children no longer danced or shouted; all stood – together with the mothers and sisters – in silence, driven together in a pack. Below, boy cleared the stairs and now dragged the anchor across the final path before the docks. Blood ran down his hands and dried along his arms in the scalding sun. Crossing onto the wooden planks of the docks, the anchor ceased its shrieking and began to dully thud against each board. The relative quiet gave Palinkos his first chance to communicate across the aquatic void. “My boy, what are you doing?” he shouted, standing up from his stool. “Why bring this instrument to the sea? It may have the look of a sea-faring tool, but that thing has no business out there!“ He knew he had been heard, but his plea was met with only thudding monotony. “You’re a fool to believe her. Look where it got your father.” At this, the boy finally stopped his relentless charge. He made not the slightest turn of head nor twitch of arm, keeping the anchor motionless in front of him. Palinkos seized the moment, “What, dear Soilson, do you hope to achieve? The farm is gone, your father too. A watery grave will help no one, least of all you.” A moment passed as the sea inhaled. “A gift,” the boy said, turning to Palinkos with a forced smile, “I wish to give a gift.” With his head again directed down at his grip, he tensed his forearms and continued the slog. Palinkos sunk back onto his stool. At the end of the dock, the boy rolled the anchor onto a small dingy and set out to sea.

He kept a strong, even pace over the sea. On any other day the rowing would have been grueling, but today, following his morning labors, Yori welcomed the respite. His earlier theatrics stuck as a fleck of embarrassment to his ribs. The heed he had not paid the old woman, who he knew as a friend of his grandfather, bothered him gravely until he forced himself to re-focus on the task at hand. A great expanse of open water lifted behind his back – and increasingly before him – and he wondered whether the dingy had the legs for the journey. The boat had been his uncle’s and, even in its younger years, had never been more than a bundle of loosely affiliated wooden pieces. Now in the light of day he could see the decay and woodworm holes eating at the hull. Yori made certain not to pull the oars toward the sky while rowing for fear of dislocating the oarlocks that tenuously held to the gunwale. The warm sea breeze channeled through his tattered shirt. He rowed some time longer, lost in unthought, first southeast and then south, until the sun burnished directly overhead. Away from the reefs and sand banks, the Mother Sea swelled before him. Here, she swaddled him. With methodical hands, he drew out a thin rope stored beneath his feet and secured one end around the eye of the anchor. Dotingly, he laid the anchor flat against the thwarts so that the metal weight hung parallel to the sea. Yori then turned around and braced himself for the task ahead. He draped his body over the anchor, the surprisingly icy metal rod chasing down his spine from nape to tail bone, planting his feet on the boards below. The pain as he lowered himself down was clear, like kneeling on a stone floor during prayer. He took the remaining rope and began wrapping it around his chest then under the anchor in tight, even circles. After four passes, he sinched down the rope like a nautical corset, stealing expansion from his lungs. The work was slow. Passing the rope from hand to hand under the increasingly taught binding sapped energy from his arms and core. He only felt satisfied once the wrapping covered from his chest down to his upper thigh. The bar, still cold, bisected his back and helped numb the ache of laying in such discomfort. The numbing spread down his limbs and up through his spine, and for a fraction of a moment, he experienced a cool paradise as the sun bled overhead. Remembering himself, Yori came back to his body. He inhaled a cool jet of sea air, then he pushed off hard with his right foot against the boards, attempting to roll him and the anchor over the gunwale. Despite his effort, the anchor barely lifted. Again, he drew up strength and shoved his might down to kick off, yet the cord around him kept any remaining power from his trunk from aiding him. Distant from the moment of calm seconds ago, he let out a visceral growl. Yori lay there for twenty heartbeats, panting like a sodden dog, the day’s physical stresses catching up with him. He hooked his fingers through the oarlocks with arms outstretched and began rocking the dingy from side to side. The motion required him to put his weight on one side of the anchor before quickly shifting to the other, forcing his vertebrae to grind back and forth over the rod. Momentum grew, and soon he could see the water again by craning his neck. He, the boat, the oars, and anchor tossed their collective weight in manic sweeps. One of the oarlocks ripped from the wooden gunwale, so he clung to the edge as best he could to continue the swing. Swirling tides and gusts pitched the boat sideways. Just as the dingy would reach its apex –just as he could picture crashing into the water below – it would roll over once more. Metal scraped bone. The oars slid out. Back again to the left, until he and the anchor hung on to gravity’s precipice. He stared at the blue directly below to his left, then the boat took one last furious rock and threw the two companions into the depths. II Light curled at odd trajectories under the water’s surface. Rays hit the surface and wound around one another before shooting off unpredictably. Tiny microbes fell off the tangled light and clustered together as drifting stars. Yori reached out to touch one of the descending starfalls and found it surprisingly heavy. Not cumbersome but similar in heft to a skipping stone. With the starfall in hand, he felt a shock of overwhelming hunger. Its weight and size and smoothness reminded him of a fig fruit, plucked by the hands of his sister and tossed down from the branches above. Starving, he plopped the starfall into his mouth. The gift was salty, brined in the juices of the sea, and delightfully chewy, moreish. His father would love them, he decided, for he cherished all things salty. His mother would ponder how to make one. ‘Equal parts salt and light,’ she would have guessed, ‘But that suppleness will be difficult to recreate.’ He thought to bring them a sample as he shoveled more of the treats into his mouth.
As they sank alongside the boy and anchor, the starfalls began crashing into one another. Upon impact, they fused into larger globules: First as figs, then oranges, then ballooning melons. The light fought to break free of these growing spheres. Spurts of raw energy flared out like fingers stretching through rolled dough. In his stomach, Yori could feel the weight of the starfalls he’d eaten. They burgeoned for space within him. His innards, though bound by rope, began to expand. He looked around, sinking deep below the surface, as the array of miniature suns quivered ever more violently, both within and without. In his gut, the energy fought to escape. Suddenly, one of the massive balls before him exploded in a flash of harsh light. The sudden release sent a shockwave reverberating through the water. A second shot off, then a third, until the entire sea ignited in a brilliant phantasmagoria. His stomach flipped, and out of his mouth, cascading from recesses wormed within, poured a flood of dense starmatter. Vomit flew to join the blinding display, even as an artifact lingered. When the light began to fade – eaten by the sea – he could no longer see the surface above. Color sapped from the water. Goose-pimples speckled his arms. The infinite sea closed in around them as they drifted down.

Thwomping woke him. His heavy eyes curled open, then shut again in protest. The sun careened through the branches above and onto his hiding spot among the roots. Sweat dried on his chest. The taste of overripe date stickied his mouth. Lifting his head, Yori could see the axes swinging against a poor tree. A man broke away from the group to approach him. “Come. We let you rest. Now we must begin again.” Yori made no motion to rise. “Stefan, we cannot do this. There must be another way.” “No. They all must go. The seer presaged its return. We cannot tempt fate, pox or no pox.” The man’s cowardice irked him. His sense of piety was all devotion and no faith. Were the opportunity to arise, the foreman would surely sell his own child for only a glimpse of heaven. “There’s still a chance it hasn’t spread. Let’s burn the blighted roots. It can be done, as my father said. With care, we could try again.” “You would test their fury? Truly? Have you learned nothing?” Stefan’s eyes narrowed in astonishment, twizzling the base of his axe into the soil. “The poison spreads. We are already too late. The Castigants have made themselves clear.” “We won’t have a harvest again for a century. Stefan, you know this.” “Yori, the village teeters. Our last dedication was paltry. The worst showing in my lifetime. To raze the orchard is the only hope we have at benediction.” Steffan’s leathered hand, outstretched in goodwill, floated before him. Ignoring the offer, Yori ambled to his feet. “What good was our last gift if now we destroy the reward?” “Only the gods know. Let’s be done with this.” Yori scowled as he strode past Stefan, heading in the direction of the work team. The two crossed through the field toward the olive orchard. On the ground lay the carcasses of ancient trees. Their twisted bodies bled black and yellow soup. He picked up an axe, and they worked at destroying his father’s orchard. One tree to the next.

A layer of jam draped over his thoughts. His fingers were freezing. Where am I? The rope had slid up – or he down – so that the fibers cut into his armpits. His dead arms dangled at his sides. Several moments passed before he remembered what had happened. Moving through a sodden dream, he raised his hands and rubbed his eye sockets and temples hoping to move the blood within him. He tried to distract himself from the tiny needles of rope fiber which dug into his arms. The discomfort was made worse by the chilling water; each fathom further numbing his fingers and toes. Frozen upon arrival. That’s a grim fate. The expanse of sea had only greyed during his sleep. His sense of space wavered. Those hands – his hands? – before him appeared alarmingly close. The occasional school of fish darted past without paying him heed, but the waters were otherwise barren. Discomfort eventually settled into indifference. The cold water alleviated the sore tension in his shoulders after hauling the anchor, and the prickle of the rope dissipated from his mind, replaced by the autonomic calm of sensory deprivation. Away from the surface they fell, escaping the trials of what came before. Yori’s mind wandered back to the anchor, and he found – inexplicably in his belly – that its continued presence made him chuckle. “Fancy a fish dinner, sister-weight? I see a flurry to our left that could make for a filling meal. Tuna, perhaps? Spookfish? You might have noticed, I lost my lunch earlier, and we didn’t pack road rations, did we?” Looking around at the empty sea before him, he fought fatigue, “Doubt we could nab anything this deep anyway.” No response came from the hunk of metal. In the far distance, the grey mirage of shifting water he’d spotted earlier grew in intensity. Sickly movements pushed the remaining light sideways in patterned steps. The water on all sides began pounding – pump, pump, pump – in sluggish rhythm. Thrusts of sea water rolled out in waves as deep, primitive harmony. In the faint light, Yori could not make out the source of the symphony, but he could feel its presence approaching. His own heartbeat joined in concord as a new fear stuck to the walls of his stomach. Regret. He no longer hungered. Out of the greying waste, a creature propelled into view. Its skin, if it could be called skin, was without color or texture, without a shell to keep it together. Within the non-skin floated guts – veins, organs, nerves wound in a knotted mess around a pounding core, a heart which drove the being forward in uniform thrusts. A web of sinew dragged behind the pulsing internals. The entire mass distended and shrank with each heartbeat. The metal shaft of the anchor began to prickle as the creature pulsed directly before him. The sensation reminded him of a hand running down his back. As the mass moved along, the thrumming continued to grow. The sea to his right folded and curled like a curtain of rain. He struggled to turn his head, so tightly was he bound, yet out of the corner of his eye he saw them begin to take shape; dozens of beating hearts in a uniform wall pushing toward him. Their pounding marched. Demanded tempo. The water vibrated, bent, and churned around him and the anchor. Time slowed to match the cadence. Blood flowed. Neurons fired. Muscles contracted. They arrived to him as a singular entity, their drum now within him. The anchor buzzed against him with electrical excitement. Sinew drifted as lost ether in every direction ignorant of the pounding flesh. One strand, milky white and pliant, brushed against the his chest. A jolt shot through him and the pounding halted. The closest mass before him rotated the sinew around its corporal center, sending further strands onto him. As they landed on his limbs, trunk, and head, each strand funneled energy through his skin and into the muscle below. His body – still lashed to the metal anchor – began to spasm at a regular cadence. The other hearts slowly pulsed toward him. The sea pulses. To live as one does not mean we die as one. There is still the scent of the fig in bloom. Sinew wormed onto him from every direction. The muscles of his legs seized. His hands contorted. A beam of sheer pain ran down the length of the anchor and surged into his back.

III They landed headfirst; the sandy sea floor took him by surprise. Slowly, the anchor fell flat against the floor without a sound. Yori, in total darkness, lay splayed like a drowned starfish, sucking air down in deep, wounded breaths. Electrical current traced throughout his body. Tiny convulsions – in his shoulders, neck, and back – wormed beneath his skin and through his nerves. The effect looped again and again, lingering for eternity, then for just a moment. Finally, with the shocks gone, his shoulders drooped back to rest on the sturdy anchor. The metal stock relaxed him, two solid arms onto which he could collapse. Sodden rope resisted his initial attempt to untie himself, so he strained to lift his arms to the knot tied around the anchor’s eye. The effort left him dizzy and he collapsed down. On his second attempt, the knot gave way. To loosen the lashing around his chest, Yori had to crunch his stomach and lift the entire anchor – made possible by their buoyancy – while also pulling the rope through. The motion, which he had to do several times, rammed his bruised coccyx into the anchor’s shaft. With three fingers width of slack, he managed to shimmy himself out of his self-imposed noose. His back meat felt raw. The muscles of his right leg cramped badly. I’ll have to drag that as well. Though it made no difference, he closed his eyes and pressed his fingers into the inflamed tissue along his thigh. For thirty heartbeats he stood, investigating the curdled muscles as a lover would. Then, eyes open, he tied a bowline through the anchor’s eye once more, threw the rope over his shoulder, and started a final, plodding march along the nightfloor.

The rain had squelched the olive tree bonfires. They watched the downpour from under a canopy where once they had pressed the olives into oil. Their work, days of manic labor cutting down the orchard, the product of a half-dozen generations worth of toil, had been rejected. Now the sickly trees wasted away in the mud. No sacrifice would reach the inhalation of their rancid gods. Yori stared blankly at the wasteland before them, his arms and face covered in resinous slime. Beside him, his sister and brother-in-law kneeled in a heap of discarded harvesting nets. “I’m sorry, Yori,” Stefan spoke up. “There is nothing to do. We will try again when the wood dries.” Ophelia stood in defiance at her husband’s contrition, “The trees decay faster than your faith, husband.” At that, the burly man sunk deeper onto the mesh. “This is mania. We cannot appeal to gods that would make us suffer. Yori, were wrong to seek indulgence from these tyrants.” Ophelia spat on the ground. “Castigation. We castigate ourselves with this servitude.” She turned toward Yori and approached her brother from behind. “No. We must broker a deal with Mother Keeper. And soon. This blight crawls toward the village border. Those fisherwives and drunks are not prepared.” Yori surveyed their land. The unlit olive bonfires were only the latest ruination. The once rich soil had been dredged of verve years ago. Runnels of rainwater split the earth as they slalomed around caked anthill mounds. The fig grove, his favorite, had long since been converted into ramshackle housing for the workers. Such waste. Ophelia grabbed his shoulder and spun him around. “Look at me!” And he did. With care, she stuck two mud-crusted, V-shaped fingers into the air between them. The gesture forced him to abandon his shock as he met her meaning. Slowly, eyes reflecting the deluge before her, never breaking contact with his own, Ophelia plunged her two fingers upside down in an arching motion, then lowered them before her face, further down over her torso and knees – now she knelt – until they stuck like two figurines in the sodden earth.
The sequence birthed a rise in him. It stuck in his throat as he struggled to keep his eyes locked with his sister’s. “You will follow father, Yori. And I will guide you.”

Dense sand grabbed at his dragging foot. Fear of sinking into the seabed kept him moving down an increasingly steep dune. The anchor kept pace behind him as it cut through the dune, frictionless. They skulked through the harsh darkness – a preview into pure deprivation, blank purgatory. Yoni made to speak, failed at that, and reckoned with the great silence around him. In his mind, instead, an entreaty: Father, there is darkness here. Tar of soil. Rusted iron and rotten lumber. It fills me, now. Did Ophelia know where she would take us? Or had you taken this secret with you as well? Forgive me, father. I did not know it would be this difficult. When their slope began to even out, he looked over his head to check the rope which, somehow, had not frayed. His wandering mind did not immediately register that he could see the braided fabric. An anemic glow before him gave shape to the rope and surrounding seabed. His body looked sickly in the vague light: Muscles misshaped, bones protruding, the trace of hair stretching from between his nipples to his groin pitifully lost on a body so wan. Yori hobbled over to the anchor, satisfied, and patted its stock, “Almost there. You can rest when we arrive. I promise.” He sighed, then headed toward the source of the light. A path grew from the naked seabed and narrowed until they reached, at the meeting point of two obsidian curtains, a gaping cavern entrance. Unruly sand evened out to flagstone as he approached. The light, now harsh to his eyes, took on a greenish hue and threw swimming shadows onto the black curtains enveloping them. At the entrance Yori hesitated only a moment, then limped through the gash at the bottom of the sea. Down, centered at the base of the cave, he saw a pool, green as unripe quince and perfectly circular at ten lengths across, that lay surrounded by a trove of misplaced items: Elaborate silver serving trays, a puffy red dress, scimitars and axes and clubs, unrecognizable instruments with dozens of strings, a spherical map the size of the anchor, a metal contraption with wheels and a strange seat, as well as hundreds of other objects he could not recognize. The spectacle transfigured in the shifting light, highlighting different areas of the bizarre collection with each passing moment. Yori thought to move forward, closer to the pool, but, with frustration, could not find his balance. A drunk nausea, spurred by the surrounding menagerie, kept him in place. He sought to ground his vision by focusing on a singular item on the wall. The map sphere, dun in hue, felt safe enough, yet as he locked his gaze closer to the curiosity, it started to rotate, first slowly, then rapidly, bloating itself along a sidelong axis. The motion captured his attention, just as it confused his perception. The walls of the cavern moved in, folded out, fell as boulders, crawled toward him. For a while – what must have been long, yes? – he resisted. Sensed himself close to fainting. Closed his eyes and crumpled onto the floor.

Behind her battery of eyes, Acheiropoita, Mother Keeper, watched the human struggle. His skin was marred by lines of discolored abrasion. Fabric and hair clung to his limbs. She could feel the vibrations of his labored breath, as well as the resonance given off by the silver object behind him. That intrigued her. Slowly, she slithered her arms down from her roost above the pool. Feeling her descent, he opened his eyes. The boy’s heartbeat betrayed his fear, though the human only shrunk back slightly in defense of his toy. Sensing the change, Mother coiled her arms in spiraled mounds before the human in a silent offering of peace. “I’ve come to give a gift,” the human cried, drowning out his obvious discomfort. “You come for absolution,” she retorted. The membranous tissue and teeth she used to speak felt inflexible in their disuse. Another stricken child. “Rest a moment. Your haste is a poor substitute for certainty.” Unwinding from her roost, she descended before the human.

Yori froze. His bravery had run its course; this thing was not what he had expected. The body, twenty-lengths tall, was of twisted pink tissue and covered in splotchy black whiskers. Mounds of pulpy flesh traced upwards in a vulgar cone. A dozen tentacles splayed from the base of the body, half serving as warped legs and half as cloying arms, two still coiled before him. Mounted toward the top, like a ram’s horn on a wall, was a plate of treasures – animal pelts, human limbs, and fossilized arthropods. The plate decorated the regions between its hundreds of black, leering eyes. And at their base, appended without care, the head of his father, shuttered and sickly pale, dangled at an off angle. The sight filled his breast with disgust. “How do you come to this place?” asked the beast, the words mangled together as produced by unhuman organs. “My father,” he stumbled, “Journeyed here before. I saw it in his dreams.” “You can see its dreams? Another’s dreams? Walkers, you serve the sea in unusual patterns. So separate. So not. That is a gift.” “Will you let me make an offering?” Yori trembled. “Terrestrial matters. My domain remains here.” “Do you not sway the others? We suffer under the Castigants’ blight. Our land is gone, as with everything else. I ask you, Mother Keeper. My weight is yours to keep,” he unnecessarily shouted while motioning to the anchor behind him. “It will serve your collection well, keeper.” “She must stay. The den captures no reward from weight, though. You, walker, misunderstand time’s collection. Your tools are tokens. Swallowed memory of transition. Your companion will remain. We will watch.” He knew to expect this – Ophelia had told him she would remain behind, a final offering in the family graveyard – yet the journey had clouded his mind. “You will end their campaign?” Yori begged. “Great Mother, you would make things right?” “Make things right? Right? Ignorance, walker. You walk blighted by ignorance. Those meddlers propagate seeds sown by inattention. You stride outside the path. There is no sacrifice through the beyond pool. The gift is taken.” “But it is received? Isn’t it!?” The boy quaked. The grotesque monster’s eyes fluttered rapidly. Then, grabbing unseen hooks on the cavern ceiling, it swung toward the boy with terrible speed. There was no time for him to react. It wrapped one of its hairy tentacles tightly around him and hoisted him into the air close to its contorted face, near the carcasses attached to the bizarre plate of trophies. Another tentacle drew up next to him. The monster, prying at his head, forcibly opened his jaw, and slithered itself down his esophagus.

Her search would be quick. Mother Keeper resented her role in this process, but it was necessary. Her grotesque children had fostered this suffering, and for their culpability, she would grant the walker’s request. She dug through his entrails and beyond his scarred tissue until she found the Salt Mistress’ residue. There, she lingered, lightly pressing on the starfall.

As the rubbery tissue dragged out from him, his body reflexively tried to purge, but the full-body retching produced only bile in his throat. Spindly arms set him down on the cavern floor where he curled himself into a coughing fit. Almost coyly, the monster slunk beside the swirling pool looking exhausted. “Stride forward. As tramped your kin. Self-see the gulf between consideration and appreciation. New burdens weigh you.” He did not much understand the meaning of the jumbled words, his mind still perturbed by the horror of what just happened. Slouched on his stool, Old-man Palinkos had spent the day catchlessly casting his rod into the sea. His rear ached from the immobility, and the intermittent splash lathered him in a layer of salt. Now, the sun ran against the horizon, hurried in its passion, as twilight settled in its wake. A seagull drifted about overhead and he motioned to the bird with a piece of bread left from his uneaten lunch. The bird squawked in dismay but circled closer all the same. “Somebody should eat it, bird. And you’ve pestered me long enough. Come, dine on my misery.” Down it swooped, landing a few paces from Palinkos. “Smarter than you look,” he proffered, tossing the bread to the gull. As the bird claimed its prize, Palinkos waited for the last drops of sun, preparing himself for the climb. And for the scene he’d have to describe once home. Left arm hooked around his folded stool and right hand grasping his rod, he made to move across the slick slate, clambering over the Salt Mistress’ rear hump onto the beach below, when he noticed the salt holes, curiously dry and coated in a vicious brine. “Wet pocks throughout the day; dry by impending night. What do you make of that?” Palinkos queried, vaguely speaking to the seagull which had, he realized, taken off while his back had turned landward and now cried a wicked cry out over the cove. “Bah,” he waved at the bird dismissively, and – nearly returning his gaze up to the stone steps – instead gandered beneath the creature in the direction of its newfound ire. There in the water, just discernable in the fading twilight, careening against the dock foundations, a body.

Behind his eyes, green floaters spun. Such ordered pattern, he thought. As if he lay covered on the beach with a cloth over his face, light piercing, shapes close, eyelashes oversized. A vision of disassociation. The immediacy bound him in its arms and warmed against the frigid ether. Clawing, prickling frission running about his limbs. A belly ache of hot porridge. A nap, long run its course, sideling into presence. A gasp. The seawater retched from his lungs like a trawl net scraping the ocean floor. As the liquid rushed out, gravity sunk in. His eyes squirreled like the balls of tissue they were. Blood thumped, once and again. Then air, sharp as shrapnel. He breathed long into himself.

Hauling the boy would be easier if the lad would stop bawling, Palinkos thought to himself. From the moment he dragged the floundering soul from the beach, he had yet to stop an animalistic, half-drowned sob. His body – flaccid, pruned, nightmarishly light – shook in spastic heaves as Palinkos carried him over a shoulder, first across the beach, then to the bottom of the steps. His own emotion, initially shaken by the distraught miracle before him, now gave way to the momentous physical task ahead. Lose yourself later, seaman. They arrived to the stone stairs, and Palinkos nearly laughed at the image in his head of a decrepit sailor climbing the village path with his misbegotten catch. As foolish as it is tragic. He continued all the same, up the stairs he had mocked earlier in the day, noting how each felt mighty tall. Not one suitable for a mouse.
