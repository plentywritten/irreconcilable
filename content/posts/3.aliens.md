You notice the colors first. Tangerine orange against lapis; vermillion against daisy yellow. The shades enwrap each man and his carapace of beetle-like armor as he enters the field of green. Supervisory figures wears stripes of zebra hide, their greyscale in obvious distinction to abundant polychrome. At either edge of the field, beyond the painted hectares, stand neon-yellow metal tongs bifurcated to the sky, as the sky rests bluer than the day it was born. The colors twist and fold, over and behind as men jockey across the constructed terrain. There they smash, sprinting, into their opposite partner, their colors overlaid in a competition of visual noise you cannot ignore. It is fabulous.  

My minder during my visit, a honey-tongued American named Chris (like their god), called the contest a "game", though I see little that could be considered a game in the exercise. From my vantage, far above the buzzing spectators, conspicuously secluded in a well-adorned room, I struggled to ascertain any elements of “play” commonly associated with how the Americans define the word “game”. The participants certainly do not appear to be having much fun. They are gargantuan, the men, with wiry beards and painted faces, standing a head taller and weighing two or three times my average human size. Atop their massive frames, they wear a shell of molded plastic that accentuates their torsos and shoulders. Below the waist, they don tights that bulge with padding tucked into pockets around the hips, thighs, and knees. A sleek, rotund helmet completes the armor package, almost growing out from the plastic shell, up over the back of the head, closing in over the cheeks and forehead but leaving room for vision and natural breath behind a stringy cage. 

Wearing these ensembles, the men throw themselves together. They collide at regular intervals, battling for territory, hunting an oblong ball. Violence is essential to this end. The men grapple and toss a partner opposite them in jabs of activity, then just as quickly, rest next to one another, seemingly amicable to the enemies around them. Then again, they strike. They tuck and dive, whirl before their partner in what might be mistaken for a choreographed performance. The men do, truly, fight (of this I have been assured), but the predictable cadence of action, its frequent stops and starts, belies authenticity. I detect a degree of verisimilitude, which I suspect may be purposeful. The Americans desire an aesthetic of combat without undercutting the game’s value as entertainment. It would be uncouth for them to enjoy bloodshed. But dressed in theatrical finery, the combat is elevated beyond base savagery, into the realm of culture. It helps that each side of the game brings a considerable entourage to the competition, many of whom, decked to the hilt in armor, stand idly on the edge of the field for the full duration of the game. Their presence adds to the verisimilitude; this conflict is not full-out warfare. When I raised this point with Chris, the man looked perplexed. “Those men would kill to win,” he stated flatly. Then, he turned me around to the extravagant buffet behind us and suggested I try a hot dog, a delicacy specific to these to these events.
